1. Background Information

1.1 Team Structure & Scope of Report

This report will document the development of the front and rear suspension outboard components, as well as regenerative braking improvements (regen) on the R26e race car. The developments fall under the chassis side of the team, specifically suspension.

In terms of functions, front outboard is primarily responsible for steering and (a large portion of) braking, rear outboard is primarily responsible for acceleration and (a smaller portion of) braking, while the friction brakes and regenerative braking (regen) straddle in between front and rear.

Vehicle dynamics is not the focus of this report, but basic understanding of some VD concepts will be required to justify the design decisions made. Brake rotor design also will not be covered, as that will be the scope for my final report.


1.2 Result From Last Season

R25e finished 7th out of 86 teams at the FSAE Michigan competition, a stellar result that tied with the team’s all-time best performance. Yet, it shared much of its suspension mechanical design with the previous car, R24e. This is due to the short development cycle, as well as designs becoming increasingly matured. Small incremental changes are safe, but stagnation is also a possibility.

I intend to overhaul the component designs for this season, trading potential testing time for the opportunity to implement different ideas that push past the bottlenecks. Many of the ideas have been thrown around in suspension departments even with the previous cohorts, but they were not considered seriously. Given the time crunch, my new designs will not drastically differ from that of my seniors, but I hope that experiences gained in trial and error can provide valuable insights for future generations of suspension designers.



2. Problem Definition

2.1. Front Outboard

R25e’s front outboard design was unable to achieve a higher sustained percentage of Ackermann steering [appendix 1] due to packaging constraints of the steering linkage, although Ackermann was identified as a design goal as it has the potential to provide greater cornering performance for the type of tracks our cars must negotiate.

2.2. Rear Outboard

R25e’s rear outboard design could not be downsized further, even though the load requirements are far below the load capacities of the components. This is due to a combination of design and component choices, and as a result there is unfulfilled potential for weight savings.

2.3. Brakes

R25e’s regen capabilities are not maximized, as the braking system had no means of actuating only the front brakes. The loading of the brake pedal was also suboptimal due to the use of extreme brake bias settings.



3. Design Goal

The overall team goal for R26e is to decrease lap time by 10% through improved reliability. While the team goal mainly concerns the powertrain departments, I intend to contribute towards the 10% lap time reduction by designing a set of lightweight outboard components for the suspension system that satisfy geometry requirements.

The new changes would bring about higher sustained Ackermann steering percentage, which will allow us to investigate the effects of higher Ackermann percentage. Static toe was used to increase the Ackermann percentage at higher steering angles, but static toe out leads to more drag and should be used sparingly. Outboard components will be lightened, leading to lighter unsprung mass which benefits ride performance (Gillespie, 1991) [appendix 2]. Regen head room will be also improved.

5. Detailed Design

5.1. Front Outboard


The components listed above are:

1. Steering clevis

2. Brake calliper

3. Front Upright

4. Wheel speed sensor

5. Camber clevis

The challenge faced by the previous iterations of NUS FSAE race cars is that of packaging. The steering clevis is located on the front of the upright for a few reasons, and even though the morphological chart includes 4 variations of packaging, 2 are inferior based on the mounting location of the brake calliper.

This is because during a braking event, the ground will exert a force on the front tyres both in the normal direction (due to weight and load transfer) and longitudinal direction (due to friction). The resultant force points upwards and towards the rear of the car. If the brake calliper is placed on the lower trailing side of the upright, it will produce a friction force that partially cancels the force exerted by the ground, leading to lower loads on the hub and hub bearings. This will lead to lighter hub designs and longer lasting bearings. Conversely, placing the calliper on the leading side of the upright will add to the force exerted by the ground, causing higher loads. [appendix 4]

Of the other 2 possibilities, steering actuation from the front was used because it better aligned with the steering rack and allows for easier packaging. The drawback is that higher Ackermann percentage cannot be achieved, as the steering pickup point will clash with the brake rotor.



As a result, R25e’s Ackermann percentage drops to below 80% as the steering angle reaches maximum, requiring 1.5° of static toe out to compensate, resulting in additional drag in the straights and heats up the tyres excessively (Milliken, 1995).

On R26e, steering actuation is moved to the rear and the mounting of the steering clevis is integrated with that of the brake callipers. This was made possible also by a 30mm wheelbase increase, which better aligns the steering rack. Actuating steering from the rear allows for more freedom in linkage placement, and R26e manages to maintain its Ackermann percentage above 90 for the entire range of steering.


The largest hurdle to overcome is packaging. The requirement to maintain the location of the brake callipers meant that the steering clevis has little space available for mounting, and an entirely new upright had to be designed to accommodate all of the components. FEA was employed to analyse the strength of the components, which will be explained in a later segment.

The steering clevis is secured to the upright using X2 bolts and blind threaded holes, as opposed to bolts and locknuts with through holes. The upright was designed with machining in mind, therefore the profile of the upright as well as any lightening cuts are aligned with the principal axes as much as possible.


5.2. Rear Outboard

The components listed above are:

1. Rear hub

2. Wheel dowel

3. Tripod liner

4. Brake button

Similar to the front outboard components, the design of the rear outboard components on NUS FSAE cars have remained largely unchanged for the past few seasons. However, regen has matured to the point where rear brakes are becoming increasingly over-specced, and the weight saving efforts are also blocked by the minimum size of wheel bearings available.

Even on a car with no regen capabilities, majority of the braking is performed by the front axle due to load transfer. When paired with regen, a rear-wheel-drive car like R25e is able to produce enough braking torque on the rear axle without ever activating the friction brakes.

When we examine Motec data from testing, we can see that the rear rotor only heats up to about 100C with regen implementation, which is covered in the next segment. Realistically, the rear brakes will only be heavily leaned upon during the brake test, which removes the need to consider the effects of thermal strain.

The rear hub bearings on R25e are angular contact ball bearings [appendix 5] with ID of 70mm and OD of 100mm. These bearings are used in pairs to provide rigidity against moments generated by the tyres due to cornering and normal forces. On paper, the load capacity of these bearing far exceeds the loads that R25e can generate during operation, yet we are not able to downsize the bearings as the hub cannot be further shrunken (at least not with the existing design).

The rear brake rotors are also oversized, which is caused by the large wheel bearings and the choice of calliper. Radially mounted ISR callipers were used for their lightweight, but the mounting mechanism forced the callipers to be mounted further away from the wheel centre, necessitating a larger diameter rotor. On R25e, the rear rotor diameter was 188mm.

To solve this problem, a new hub design is being implemented where the profile of the hub is cut down to the bare minimum without clashing with the half shaft tripod. This lowered the required ID of the hub bearing to 60mm, but the design does generate some concerns regarding the strength of the preload plate. An extensive study on the design of the preload plate was then conducted, resulting in a slightly heavier but sufficiently strong design. The simulation setup will also be covered later.

NSK is currently a sponsor of the NUS FSAE team, and they offer bearings of the specified size in their catalogue. The bearings also exceed the load specifications, but further downsizing would require completely different methods of transmitting torque to the outboard. For now, NSK’s 7912 series are the smallest hub bearings that can be used.

A new style of calliper is also used. Axially mounted AP callipers (of the same overall weight) further reduced the required size of the rotor, resulting in a reduction in diameter from 188mm to 172mm. The design of the rotors will not be covered in the interim report.

All of the component and design changes resulted in significant weight savings, which will be covered in the segment on design outcomes.

5.3. Brakes and Regen

R25e marks the first time that regen became effective. The regen logic works as such:

1. Determine the ideal brake balance at various speeds.

2. Bias the brakes to the front, which results in a shortfall in rear brakes line pressure.

3. Compute the amount of regen required to match that ideal brake bias at that given speed and send negative torque command to motor.

However, there are still teething issues with the system, both in terms of regen efficiency and physical design. Due to the design of the balance bar, it is not possible to divert all of the braking force to the front axle, resulting in the rear brakes being actuated even though regen is capable of braking the rear axle on its own. As mentioned in the previous segment, some heating of the rear rotor is still observed, which means that regen has not recuperated all of the available energy.

In order to send the most braking force to the front and maximize regen, extreme bias settings were also used. This caused twisting in the brake pedal box assembly, and even though the components designed by our team survived the season thanks to rigorous FEA, the ones bought off the shelf are much worse for wear.

R26e will use an electronic shutoff valve, which when energized cuts off hydraulic pressure to the rear. It is a normally open valve, so that in the event of low voltage system failure, the system will continue to allow fluid flow. When the driver presses the brake pedal and front line pressure builds up, the valve will be activated. This will stop the pressure in the rear line from increasing, allowing for more regen.

Additionally, a more neutral balance bar position can be used, and twisting of the pedal can be reduced. Master cylinder choices will also be opened up, as the old car also relied on bore sizes of master cylinders to strike a balance between still being able to pass brake test and being able to send more braking force to the front. With the ability to cut off the rear brakes, the hydraulics can be designed to optimize for more equal loading of the pedal box instead.

7. Limitations

There are some potential drawbacks to the new designs, as previously described by the scoring table. The front suspension has much tighter packaging, which can make installation of parts tricky. However, this will only become a problem during initial assembly and in the event of a rebuild, hence the impact is still minor.

The new rear hub design allows for significant weight savings, but manufacturing and assembly also becomes more complicated. Specifically, bearing tolerance is something that needs to be controlled more tightly, as excessively tight press fit can cause much more damage to the islands compared to a hub that has the full circumference of the profile. As such, a tight sliding fit of h6 is called for.

The addition of the shutoff valve brings with it more hydraulic connections, which are point of failures. The NPT inlet and outlet [appendix 7] that come with the shutoff valve can be difficult to install correctly due to the tapered threads, and utmost care is required to ensure that there is no leak.

Appendix 1: Ackermann

Ackermann steering percentage is a measure of how well the vehicle’s steering follows the Ackermann steering geometry. 100 percent Ackerman takes place when all of the wheels on the vehicles travel on paths that are concentric to the sane point without any of the wheels scrubbing. This assumes that there is very low slip angle at all wheels.

The actual computation of Ackermann percentage will not be covered here, as it is much easier to use vehicles dynamic softwares such as OptimumK to calculate the value. The only takeaway is that Ackermann can describe the difference between how much each of the front wheels are steered.

Pro-Ackermann steering tends to be used for race cars that have to traverse technical tracks where turn radii are tight, and corner speeds are low. Examples include hill climb racing and FSAE. Yet, when consulting the giants in the race car engineering space as to what is the best, many of the gurus also disagree on what should be the correct answer. Carol Smith concluded that the racing car should probably have parallel steering, and that it is not very critical anyway (Smith, 1978). On the other hand, Lars Frömmig argues that the type of Ackermann should depend on the tire behaviour. If the peak lateral force of the tyre is achieved at the same angle across all normal loads, parallel steering is desirable. If the ideal slip angle increases with normal load, then anti-Ackermann should be used to steer the outside wheel more and produce that higher slip (since that wheel will be more loaded). Lastly, tyres that have decreasing ideal slip angle with increasing normal load favour pro-Ackermann (Frömmig, 2023). It is also worth noting that our tyres supposedly exhibit increasing ideal slip angle as normal load increases.

It would then be hard for me to draw any conclusion other than that the ideal Ackermann percentage would be influenced by type data and vehicle dynamic models, as we need to predict the load that each tyre will experience and match the tyres with the correct slip angles.

Without a reliable method of measuring slip angles and tyre loads on the actual vehicle, we have to use intuition. At the speed that we traverse the track, and given the composition of the track, it would require a few things:

1. Large steering input. Tight corners simply mean that drivers have to steer more, and the effects of Ackermann percentage will only become evident at higher steering input.

2. Low speeds. Unless intentionally forced / unintentionally miscalculated, it is hard to induce large slip angles on the car given how slow we are going (max 70kph and average 45kph).

3. Strong initial turn-in. The lack of long sweeping corners and the high frequency of tight manoeuvres prioritize faster directional change over sustained cornering capabilities.

All of this is to say that we would like to experiment with higher Ackermann percentage to investigate its effects on lap time, and that we are setting pro-Ackermann steering geometry as a design goal.

Appendix 2: Unsprung mass

It is better to minimize the unsprung mass on a vehicle as this reduces road-to-body transmissibility (Gillespie, 1991). There will be a point in the frequency range where the unsprung mass goes into resonance. This will lead to degraded road holding performance. Therefore, we want to avoid having the unsprung mass going into wheel hop, and having lighter unsprung mass helps to reduce the response gain at mid to lower frequencies, which is exactly where our car operates.

Appendix 3: Alternate design choices from morphological chart

the main roles of the front outboard components are to steer and brake. Almost all FSAE cars make use of some form of rack and pinion steering, and almost all FSAE cars make use of multi-piston fixed brake callipers. The question then becomes that of packaging, and which component should be placed at which location.

For rear outboard components (especially for a rear-wheel drive car with a mechanical differential), their main role is to transmit torque. This means that the hub must be able to receive the torque coming from the differential, then pass it to the wheels and tyres. This is easier said than done, because the hub may move in/outboard during suspension travel due to the motion of the upright as constrained by the suspension arms. This means that the hub must be abled to move in/outboard, but be constrained to the rotation of the half shaft.

In terms of how the torque is sent to outboard, tractive and cooling department has the final say. Single piece half shafts with tripod joints will be used, therefore the design of the rear hubs must be able to accommodate. Typically, the profile of the tripod cup is machined into the hub, but there are also off-the-shelf solutions such as this adaptor from Taylor Race Engineering. The reasons for integrating the tripod cup into the hub are weight and size, as a much smaller and lighter hub can be designed.

Appendix 4: Forces during braking

In order to make calculations easier, we will be representing forces in terms of G. When a braking event occurs, the ground will exert a frictional force on the tyre (purple). Our car can produce sustained braking force of 1.5 G , and for the sake of simplicity, we assume that all of this is done at the front wheels.

Weight transfer also happens when brakes are applied, and we assume that the car has transferred all of its weight to the front axle, hence 1 G of vertical force is appleid to the hub (blue). This force combine with the frictional force from the ground will give a resultant force that is about 1.8 G (green), pointed up and towards the rear.

We know that the placement of the calliper needs to be around the brake rotor, and we know that the force produced by the calliper needs to counteract the momement produced by the frictional force from the ground. Since the distance between the calliper (rotor mean diamter) is about half of that of the wheel diamter, the magnitude of the forced generated by the calliper will have to be 3 G (red).

Where we choose to place the red force will then determine how mucch load the hubs and bearings will experience, and we can only choose a location that is tangent to the brake rotor. Of all the possible locations, placing the callipers on the trailing side of the upright pointing towards the lower ball joint will producee the lowest overall force.

Appendix 5: Angular contact bearings

Angular contact bearings are a special type of ball bearings, where the inner and outter races contact that balls at an angle instead of perpendicular to the axis of rotation. These bearings are used in pairs for our case, and they can support axial loads better than standard ball bearings.

The arrangement used is back-to-back, as it provides more stability against any moment that may arise. Another reason for choosing back to back is the method of mounting, as the bearing are not installed up againts each other, and the upright only supports the bearings from inside. A face-to-face arangement would make the bearings vulnerable to axial loads.

Appendix 6: FEA results interpretation

Extensive simulations were performed on the components to ensure that they will be able to withstand the forces during the car's operation.The load cases used were set to be the theoretical maximum performance of the car, given its physical properties and tyre data. The concept of traction circle is often used to describe the performance envelope of racing vehicles, and it is possible to calculate forces from the maximum acceleration figures. However, FSAE cars are characterized by their abundance of cornering capabilities and lack of power, and the traction circles often look more like an ellipse with the positive longitudinal G being much lower than braking and cornering.

Specific to the rear hubs, the effect of tripod torque was simulated. This caused the simulation runtime to increase from 20 min per load case to over 1 hr, which is why it was only performed at the start and the end of the design process to avoid slowing down interations. This is due to the addition of multiple sliding contacts and the requirement to use fine mesh at the tripod contacts.

In our simulation, acceleration is set to be 1 G, Cornering at 2 G and Braking at -1.8 G. These are not sustainable figures, but they do show up as peaks in test run data. Spring and tyres are also not considered due to limited simulations capabilities, making the conditions even harsher. As mentioned in the main body, both the rear hub and rear preload plate could not achieve FOS 2.0, but this is under the most extreme load case where the suspension is subjected to a 3 G bump, corners at 2 G and accelerates at 1 G. This is well outside the traction circle, but the load case is still used to gauge how well the design will hold up during accidents such as curb strikes.

Appendix 7: NPT standard

NPT stands for National Pipe Thread, which is a thread standard from the US. It is characterized by its tapered thread and requires the used of PTFE sealing tape.
